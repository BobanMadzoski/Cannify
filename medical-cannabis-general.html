What is Medical Cannabis?

Medical cannabis (also known as medicinal cannabis or medical marijuana) refers to the use of the cannabis plant and its component cannabinoids (THC, CBD, CBC, etc.) as medical therapy to alleviate symptoms. There are more than 500 components found within the cannabis plant, including over 110 cannabinoid compounds, which are thought to be responsible for most of the plantâs therapeutic benefits.

Cannabinoids are found in resin, most abundant in the female flower head, which is used in the manufacture of medicinal cannabis products. These plant cannabinoids mimic the naturally occurring cannabinoids within the human body, and act predominantly through our endocannabinoid system.

Indica vs Sativa

Cannabis sativa and Cannabis indica are used today in various hybrid breedings to generate different chemotypes. These have either a high THC and low CBD content, or vice versa, a high CBD and low THC content. The third chemotype is an intermediate form with balanced concentrations of THC and CBD.

The Endocannabinoid System.

The endocannabinoid system (ECS) is a biological system of our body that can interact with endogenous cannabinoids to regulate vital processes. However, this interaction is not limited to endocannabinoids: plant cannabinoids, known as phytocannabinoids, can also interact with this system. The ECS is thus the biological basis for externally supplied cannabinoids to exert their effects in our bodies.

Treatment with Medical Cannabis.

Medical cannabis is not currently available as a first line treatment. It can only be prescribed after conventional therapy has failed or is found to be not suitable (eg adverse effects) or ineffective.

When to consider Medical Cannabis?

The research around cannabis is still growing and the products, doses and research methods used vary between studies. This makes a medical practitionerâs role complicated when it comes to advice on how to best use medicinal cannabis products.

The Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) advises that in most cases, medicinal cannabis should be used only when registered treatments have been tried and failed to manage conditions or have been unable to be tolerated.

Cannabis Law.

Medical cannabis has been used in the treatment of numerous diseases for many thousands of years. That only changed with the Opium Convention of 1929, which banned cannabis in Germany. This legal provision was only changed a few years ago. Namely with the law "passed in Germany in 2017 Cannabis as Medicine". The declared aim of the federal government is to improve the palliative treatment options for patients.

According to the cannabis law, the flowers can officially be used as medicinal products. However, the doctor must ensure that all conventional means of combating the disease or alleviating symptoms have already been exhausted. Only under these circumstances are cannabinoid drugs available to patients on a prescription.
